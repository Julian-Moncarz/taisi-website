# TAISI Website — Purpose & Goals

## What is TAISI?

Toronto AI Safety Initiative (TAISI) is a student group at the University of Toronto focused on mitigating catastrophic risks from advanced AI. Part of a network of AI safety university groups funded by Kairos — alongside groups at Oxford, Harvard, MIT, and Cornell. Partnered with Trajectory Labs, an AI safety research hub in Toronto.

> **Framing note:** Name-drop only the most prestigious unis (Oxford, Harvard, MIT, Cornell). Don't say "51 universities" — being in an elite club > being one of many. Let the names do the work.

## What is the site for right now?

One thing: **drive applications to the Summer 2026 Intensive.**

Everything else is secondary. The site may also passively build credibility for people who google "TAISI" after seeing a flyer or hearing a class pitch, but the goal for those people is the same — apply.

## Target audience

**Primary:** Technical undergrads at U of T (and possibly other Toronto universities). Typically 1st–3rd year CS, math, stats, Econ, engineering students. They want research experience, internships, and resume-building opportunities. They may or may not know what AI safety is. They can code.

**Not targeting:** Grad students, people already deep in AIS, people who can't code, anyone not in Toronto for the summer, anyone who has graduated. They can apply if they want but we are not aiming for them.

## What is the Summer Intensive?

- 4 full weekend days spread across 4 weeks (one weekend day per week for a month)
- Mornings: Intro to AI safety content (value alignment, threat models, governance)
- Lunch: Catered (free, good food), AIS researchers come by
- Afternoons: Hands-on technical upskilling workshops (build evals, use Inspect, analyze research directions) — participants produce portfolio artifacts
- Hosted at Trajectory Labs (a real AI safety research space, not a classroom)
- Free for participants. API credits provided.
- Applications close April 5th, 2026.
- Capacity: ~160 participants. Selective but not brutally so — "limited spots, rolling review."

## What do participants walk away with?

1. **Portfolio artifacts** — evals on their GitHub, published write-ups analyzing research directions. Tangible things that signal AIS research competence.
2. **Technical AIS research skills** — practical, not theoretical. They leave able to do SPAR-level research.
3. **Network** — exposure to AIS researchers, Trajectory Labs, the Kairos network. A foot in the door to a fast-growing field.
4. **understanding of what AIS is** - idk if they care about this so idk if we tell them about it, but we are trying to build it in them.
5. **A path forward** — top performers may be matched with research mentors for the rest of the summer.

## The core value proposition

**The message (not final copy):** AI safety is one of the fastest-growing research fields. Orgs are hiring. You don't know how to break in. In 4 weekends, we'll intro you to the field, teach you the skills, have you build real portfolio pieces, and connect you with researchers — free, at a professional research space, on weekends so you can keep your internship.

> **Copy note:** The message is right, the tone needs work. Final copy in the style/structure doc. The credibility question ("can we convince people we are legit?") is answered by design + partner names + program specificity, not by copy alone.

## Primary CTA

**"Apply"** — not "give us your email." The application should be short (name, email, program, year, 1–2 short-answer questions). It should feel like an opportunity, not a newsletter signup. The act of applying creates psychological commitment.

- The apply button / form should be impossible to miss on every page.
- Applications close April 5th — deadline creates urgency.
- "Limited spots" / "rolling review" — not a specific number.

## What the site needs to make someone feel

A target student (2nd year CS, has an internship lined up, curious about AI, maybe saw a poster or heard a pitch in CSC148) lands on the site. They should feel:

1. **"These people are legit."** — Kairos network (Oxford, Harvard, MIT, Cornell), Trajectory Labs partnership, professional design. This is not a club that meets in a basement. They have money and are real.
2. **"This is a real opportunity."** — Selective application, limited spots, tangible outcomes (portfolio, skills, network). This is worth 4 weekends.
3. **"I could actually do this."** — It's free, it's on weekends, you don't need to already know AIS, you just need to be able to code. Low barrier, high reward.
4. **"I should apply now."** — Deadline, rolling review, limited spots. Don't sit on it.

## Objections the site must overcome

| Objection | How the site addresses it |
|---|---|
| "I don't know what AI safety is" | 2-line explanation, max. Frame as career opportunity, not moral obligation. |
| "I don't trust students to run this well" | Lead with Kairos network, Trajectory Labs, professional venue. Don't emphasize that organizers are students. |
| "I have an internship / commitments" | Weekends only, 1 day/week for 4 weeks. Explicitly say "designed for students with existing commitments." |
| "I'm not smart enough / this isn't for me" | Position as an on-ramp. "If you can code, you're ready." (Exact copy TBD.) |
| "4 full days is a lot" | Make the ROI obvious — portfolio pieces, skills, network, research connections. |
| "This sounds like a club, not a real program" | Design, partners, selectivity, and network do the heavy lifting. The site must look professional. |

## Credibility signals (ranked by strength)

1. **Kairos network** — "Funded by Kairos, alongside AI safety groups at Oxford, Harvard, MIT, and Cornell."
2. **Trajectory Labs partnership** — "Hosted at Trajectory Labs, a dedicated AI safety research hub in Toronto."
3. **Selectivity** — "Limited spots. Applications reviewed on a rolling basis."
4. **Professional design** — The site itself is a credibility signal. If it looks amateur, nothing else matters.
5. **Program structure** — Specific program description (mornings = content, afternoons = workshops, portfolio artifacts) signals this is planned and real.
6. **Past programs** — Brief mention that TAISI has run intro fellowships (technical + governance) to show track record.

> **No logo bar.** Kairos + Trajectory alone would look thin as a logo section. Instead, weave partner names into copy naturally (e.g., "Funded by Kairos" in a credibility line, "Hosted at Trajectory Labs" in the program description). This reads as confident rather than scraping for logos.

## What is NOT on the site (scope fence)

- No team/organizer page (for now)
- No blog or resources section
- No "contact us to start research now" alternate CTA (distracts from the main funnel)
- No detailed curriculum (keep it high-level to maintain flexibility)
- Brief mention of past fellowships for credibility (not a full page — just a line like "We've run X fellowships with Y participants")
- No sponsor logo bar

## Success metrics for the site

- **Primary:** Number of completed applications
- **Secondary:** Conversion rate (site visit → application start → application submit)
- **Tertiary:** Time on site, bounce rate

> **Note:** Need analytics from day one (Vercel Analytics or similar — simple, no setup overhead).

## Traffic sources

Most traffic will come from:
1. QR codes shown during in-class presentations
2. Word of mouth / referral links
3. QR codes on flyers / posters around campus
4. Discord and mailing list shares

This means: the site must work extremely well on **mobile** (QR → phone browser). The above-the-fold experience on mobile is critical.
